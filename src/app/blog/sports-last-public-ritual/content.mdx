## Sixty thousand people feeling the same thing

When was the last time you stood in a room full of strangers and felt something together? Not performed feeling. Not the outrage you post or the enthusiasm you fake at a work offsite. Actually felt something, in your body, at the same moment as the people around you.

If you're like most Americans, the honest answer is a sporting event.

Which should tell us something. Not about sports. About everything else.

## The collective effervescence problem

Emile Durkheim, writing in 1912, coined the term *collective effervescence* for what happens when people gather and move and feel in unison. He was studying Aboriginal Australian ceremonies, but the concept travels. When human beings synchronize, chanting or singing or moving together, something happens that can't be reached alone. Individual identity dissolves for a while into group identity, and people come out reporting transcendence, belonging, meaning.

Durkheim argued this was the actual basis of religious experience. Not the theology and not the doctrine, but the physical act of gathering and synchronizing with others.

For most of human history the rituals that delivered this were everywhere: religious services, harvest festivals, civic ceremonies, union meetings, political rallies, town dances. The calendar was thick with occasions for collective feeling.

Now look around. Church attendance in the US has dropped below 50% for the first time in Gallup's tracking history. Civic organizations are hollowed out, union membership sits at historic lows, and political gatherings have curdled into polarized spectacles most people avoid. Town dances don't exist.

So what's left that still produces real collective effervescence for millions of people on a regular schedule? Sports. Pretty much just sports.

## More than a game

The standard intellectual posture toward sports fandom is mild contempt. Grown adults screaming about a ball. Tribal loyalties over laundry, as Jerry Seinfeld put it. A distraction from real problems, *bread and circuses*. The posture is wrong, and the research is the reason.

Daniel Wann, a psychologist at Murray State University, has spent decades studying fandom through his Team Identification-Social Psychological Health Model. The findings are consistent: strong team identification tracks with lower rates of depression and loneliness, higher self-esteem, more social connectedness. These aren't small effects. For a highly identified fan, the team is a real source of belonging.

And it isn't passive. Think about what a committed fan actually does. They learn the history and arcana. They build a shared language with other fans, full of inside references. They keep rituals, the specific clothes and the specific seat and the specific chant. They take on collective joy and collective grief, and they belong to something that outlasts their own small life.

Strip away the content and you've described a religious community, with its sacred spaces and holy days and saints and heresies, and at the center the experience of losing yourself in something larger. The stadium is the church, the game day the holy day, the legendary player the saint, supporting a rival the one unforgivable sin.

## The tailgate as secular communion

Consider the tailgate. People show up hours early in a parking lot. They cook and share the food, often with strangers, drink together, tell stories, wear the colors. They build a temporary community in an asphalt desert, then process together into the cathedral.

This is communion: a shared meal followed by a shared ritual. The theological content swapped out for athletic content, the social function untouched. You arrive as one isolated person and leave attached to something.

And people do it voluntarily, every weekend, across the whole country, with nobody telling them they should and no guilt if they don't. They do it because it feels good and meets a need nothing else in their week meets.

When was the last time church felt that alive? For most Americans the honest answer is never, or not in decades. The congregations that still generate this energy, Pentecostal churches, Black church traditions, megachurches with rock-concert production, tend to be the ones growing. They worked out what Durkheim worked out a century ago: the feeling is the point.

## The dark side is real

I'm not going to pretend this is all wholesome.

The shadow side is serious. The tribalism that produces belonging also produces hostility to the outgroup, and that's where you get soccer hooliganism, fan violence, and the old fusion of sport and nationalism that fed fascist spectacle and still surfaces whenever international competition becomes a proxy for ethnic or national supremacy.

Corporate capture has hollowed out the communal part. Naming rights, luxury boxes that segregate fans by wealth, ticket prices that price working-class people out of the spaces they built their identities around. The Dallas Cowboys' AT&T Stadium has suites that run $500,000 a season. That isn't collective experience. It's a class barrier with a jumbotron.

The parasocial dimension cuts both ways. Feeling connected to a team can supplement real relationships or quietly replace them, and a person whose whole social identity runs through a team on television is still, when the broadcast ends, sitting alone in their living room.

The industry around all this has gotten very good at converting the communal impulse into revenue: publicly funded stadiums that enrich billionaire owners, gambling apps built to monetize every second of attention, merchandise cycles engineered to make last year's jersey embarrassing. The need for belonging is real, and it gets farmed.

## What we lost

Whether fandom is good or bad isn't the interesting question. Its intensity is, because the intensity marks a vacuum.

If people had abundant sources of collective effervescence, if they gathered regularly in ways that produced belonging, sports wouldn't carry this much weight. It would just be entertainment, enjoyable and forgettable.

Instead, for millions of people game day is the one day they feel part of something, the stadium the one place they lose themselves in a crowd, the team the one institution they trust without reservation. That says nothing damning about the fans. It says something damning about a society that offers them so little else.

We took apart most of the institutions that used to supply collective experience and replaced them with nothing. Then we act surprised when people pour religious intensity into the few gathering rituals that survived.

## The question nobody asks

We spend a lot of energy arguing whether sports are good for society, whether athletes are role models, whether stadiums deserve public money. The better question is why this is the only thing left.

Why is a stadium the last place in American life where a hedge fund manager and a janitor sit next to each other and scream the same words at the same time? Why is Sunday football the last standing weekly ritual for millions of households? We mock people for caring about the one thing that hands them exactly what every sociologist says humans need, which is regular, ritualized, collective emotional experience.

Sports aren't the problem. The absence of everything else is. The stadium isn't too full. The calendar around it is too empty.
