## The problem with building machines

Once you build a deportation apparatus, you can't just switch it off when it becomes politically inconvenient. The machine has no off switch. It has budgets, personnel, institutional momentum, and quotas to hit.

You build the infrastructure to deport "the bad ones." Noble enough on its face: get rid of criminals, keep communities safe. But an enforcement bureaucracy is measured by throughput. By how many people it processes in a year.

And when it runs out of "bad ones," it doesn't shut down and send everyone home.

## Mission creep isn't a bug

Immigration and Customs Enforcement employs tens of thousands of people on an annual budget measured in billions. Many of the detention centers are private and profit-driven, and they need to maintain occupancy to justify their contracts.

So what happens when an organization built to find and remove people starts running low on obviously deportable targets? It expands the definition. It broadens the net. It finds new categories of people who technically qualify under increasingly creative readings of the law. None of that is conspiracy; it's how institutions behave. They justify their existence by doing the thing they were built to do, whether or not that thing still needs doing at the same scale.

The machine started with a promise. We're only going after dangerous criminals, gang members, threats to public safety. The infrastructure doesn't care about the promise. It cares about capacity utilization.

## The ratchet only turns one way

Every expansion of enforcement power gets sold as temporary. Targeted, surgical, just this one group, just until we've solved the problem. Then the powers rarely contract. The hiring doesn't reverse, the surveillance systems don't get dismantled, and the detention centers don't close, least of all the private ones carrying contractual minimum-occupancy requirements.

Each expansion becomes the new baseline instead. The floor, not the ceiling.

The next administration, whatever its party, inherits a bigger and more automated apparatus than the last one left behind. It might point the machine at different groups, or use it more gently. But the machine is already there, humming, waiting to be deployed.

## Numbers over people

The shift from targeting to quota-hitting happens gradually, then all at once. Early on, ICE agents focus on people with serious criminal records, which is easy to defend. The trouble is there aren't enough of them to keep the machine running at capacity.

So the criteria widen. Minor offenses count. Traffic violations. Being in the wrong place at the wrong time. Eventually any undocumented person is a valid target, not because the policy explicitly changed but because the institution needs to hit its numbers.

This is what industrialized enforcement does. Discretion evaporates and the system optimizes for efficiency rather than justice. You end up with farmworkers picked up during traffic stops, and parents detained while dropping kids at school, and people who've lived in the country for decades swept up because a database flagged them and the agent had a quota to meet.

Is this the system working as intended? Depends what you think the intention was.

## The private prison problem

Then there's the profit motive. Private prison companies don't make money from empty beds, and many of their contracts carry guaranteed minimums, literal requirements to keep facilities at a given occupancy or pay a penalty.

So an enforcement agency that justifies its budget by producing deportations works in tandem with contractors that need to keep their beds full. The incentives line up neatly, just not in favor of solving anything or treating people with dignity.

The machine feeds itself. More detentions justify larger budgets, larger budgets buy more capacity, more capacity creates pressure to fill the beds. Nobody had to sit down and decide to make the system crueler. The institutional logic handled that on its own.

## Automation makes it worse

You'd expect technology to make enforcement more precise. More careful, more targeted. That's not what it does here.

Automation in deportation means facial recognition at borders, license plate readers in immigrant neighborhoods, data-sharing between local police and federal authorities, algorithms that flag people on risk scores nobody fully understands. What it doesn't buy is more consideration of the individual case. It buys faster processing and higher throughput. The machine gets better at being a machine.

And once these systems are deployed, they stay. The databases don't get deleted and the surveillance infrastructure doesn't come down. Future administrations inherit not just the legal authority to deport but the technical capacity to do it at scale.

## The liberal's dilemma

This is where a certain kind of political analysis falls apart. You can't build the deportation machine on the assumption that only responsible people will ever operate it. Power doesn't work that way. Build the infrastructure and someone will use it, maybe not this election cycle, but eventually someone less concerned with procedural niceties takes the controls and finds the hard work already done for them.

The Obama administration deported more people than any previous president. It set records, built capacity, expanded the machine, and justified all of it as being "smart" about enforcement, targeting the right people and setting priorities. The next administration inherited that expanded capacity and said, in effect, thanks for the upgrade.

This isn't about individual politicians making bad calls. It's institutional logic. Once built, the machine develops its own imperatives, its own momentum, and a constituency of people whose jobs and budgets depend on it running.

## What reform can't fix

You can't reform the deportation machine into something humane. You can tweak policies, change enforcement priorities, issue memos about discretion. The infrastructure underneath stays put: the agents, the detention centers, the quotas, the profit motives, the surveillance.

As long as the machine exists it gets used, and as long as it's used it expands, and more people end up caught in its gears who were never supposed to be targets. That's not a failure of the system. That's the system doing what systems like this do, which is grow, find new targets, and justify themselves through their own activity.

## The only off switch

So what's the solution? If the machine can't be reformed, can't be trusted to stay inside its original bounds, can't be expected to shrink on its own, what's left?

The uncomfortable answer is that you don't fix it. You dismantle it. You stop trying to make mass deportation more humane or efficient, and you stop doing mass deportation.

That requires admitting the whole apparatus is the problem, not just its excesses but its core function. Which is a harder conversation than most people are ready to have. Until someone has it, the machine keeps running, because the thing about machines is that capacity, once it exists, demands to be used.
