## The illusion of neutrality

You already have a mythology. You have a story that explains where you came from, what you're supposed to do, and what happens when you die. Maybe you don't call it mythology. Maybe you call it "just the way things are" or "being realistic" or "science." It's mythology all the same.

The rationalist who scoffs at religious narratives has their own creation story (the Big Bang), their own metaphysics (materialism), their own eschatology (heat death, or technological transcendence), their own ethics (usually some variant of utilitarian calculation). These aren't empirical discoveries. They're frameworks for organizing experience into meaning.

That isn't a criticism, just the human condition. We can't operate without these frameworks. The brain can't process raw experience; it needs categories and narratives and purposes. Strip those away and you don't get enlightened rationality. You get psychological collapse.

## The meaning-seeking animal

Evolution produced a creature that asks why.

This was probably adaptive. The ability to construct causal narratives (this berry made me sick, that animal attacks when threatened, these clouds mean rain) conferred survival advantages. Pattern recognition and story construction overlap heavily in the brain. We're built to find meaning even where none exists, which is where conspiracy theories and faces in random noise come from.

The same capacity that helps us predict the weather makes us need cosmic explanation. We can't just exist; we need a reason for existing. The brain that asks "why did the branch fall?" eventually asks "why am I here?"

There's no way to turn this off. You can refuse to consciously construct meaning, but your unconscious does it anyway. You'll find yourself acting as if life has purpose even while professing that it doesn't. The professed nihilist still gets out of bed, still makes choices, still feels betrayed by injustice. The behavior reveals a meaning structure the words deny.

## The vacuum gets filled

When traditional religious meaning structures collapse, as they have across much of the developed world, the need for meaning doesn't disappear. It finds other outlets.

Politics becomes religion. So does economic theory, nationalism, self-optimization, environmental catastrophism. Not metaphorically but structurally: these frameworks provide the same services, explaining suffering, promising redemption, gathering a community of believers, naming villains, handing out a purpose to pursue.

Because they aren't recognized as religious, they often run more extreme. Traditional religions have centuries of theological development behind them, of learning to moderate claims and tell metaphor from literalism. The new secular religions have no such tradition. They're prey to every pathology of fundamentalism without the self-awareness to recognize it.

A political ideology held religiously turns fanatical. A diet held religiously turns into disordered eating dressed in self-improvement language. The religious impulse, denied legitimate expression, goes underground and comes back distorted.

## The psychological research

This isn't only philosophical speculation. The empirical literature is consistent: meaning-making tracks with psychological wellbeing.

People who report having meaning in their lives handle stress better and show lower rates of depression and anxiety. The content of the meaning matters less than having it. Religious meaning works, and so does secular purpose, whether it's a career calling, family, or an artistic mission. What doesn't work is having no meaning at all.

There's a reason addiction recovery programs emphasize higher powers. Not because supernatural beings exist (a separate question) but because humans need to locate themselves within larger narratives. The isolated individual pursuing individual pleasure is a recipe for despair. Connection to something beyond the self, something that gives structure, is closer to a requirement than a preference.

Depression is often described as meaninglessness. The experience isn't primarily sadness; it's the collapse of purpose. Nothing matters, nothing is worth doing, the future holds nothing of value. It's a meaning crisis dressed as a mood disorder. Medications can help with the neurochemistry, but lasting recovery usually requires reconstructing a sense of meaning.

## Which mythology?

If meaning isn't optional, if we're going to have a mythology whether we acknowledge it or not, the question becomes which one.

Not all meaning structures are equal. Some make people miserable, some produce terrible behavior, some collapse under their own contradictions. The medieval peasant had plenty of meaning, and that meaning trapped them in rigid hierarchies and justified horrific cruelty. Meaning isn't automatically good.

You can evaluate a mythology. Does the framework correspond reasonably well to observable reality? Does it produce flourishing for the people who hold it, or misery? Does it make demands that are actually achievable? Does it offer real community or just in-group tribalism, and does it let people face suffering and death with some dignity?

These questions matter more than whether the mythology is "true" in some naive empiricist sense. All mythologies are metaphorical at bottom. The universe doesn't literally have a purpose, but humans need to act as if it does. The choice isn't between mythology and no mythology. It's between better and worse ones.

## The failure of secular substitutes

The Enlightenment promised that reason would replace religion and supply meaning without the supernatural baggage. It hasn't worked out.

Pure reason can't generate meaning. It can analyze relationships between propositions and optimize for given goals, but it can't supply the goals themselves. "Why should I continue living?" isn't a question logic can answer, and neither is "what makes life worth living?" Both require value claims that reason can't produce from first principles.

So secular societies import meaning from elsewhere, usually from religious traditions they no longer acknowledge. Human rights, individual dignity, the moral equality of persons: none of these follow from materialism. They're borrowed from Christianity and smuggled in without attribution. The secular humanist standing on two thousand years of religious moral development while mocking religion is sawing off the branch underneath them.

And the borrowed capital is running out. Each generation receives less of the implicit meaning structure. The transmission mechanism (religious education, communal ritual, shared sacred text) has been dismantled, and what replaces it tends to be consumption or career. The thin gruel of secular psychology can't nourish like the feast of religious tradition.

## Building meaning consciously

Most people throughout history inherited their meaning structures. They were born into communities with established narratives, rituals, and purposes. Meaning was a given, not a choice.

That's less and less true. Many people now find themselves meaning-orphans, cut off from the traditions that shaped their ancestors, without obvious alternatives. They have to construct meaning deliberately.

This is both opportunity and burden. The opportunity is freedom to select a structure that actually fits, rather than being stuck with whatever you inherited. The burden is the exhausting work of construction with no communal reinforcement to lean on.

Doing it consciously means engaging the deep questions. What is a good life? What do I owe other people? How should I face suffering and death? What, if anything, outlasts me? None of these has an easy answer. They require reading, reflection, conversation, and the willingness to take seriously traditions that might seem alien or outdated. Mostly they require the humility to admit that people throughout history were not idiots, and their answers deserve consideration.

## The return to practice

Meaning isn't primarily propositional. It's enacted.

You can hold a coherent framework that does nothing for you because you never practice it. The philosophy on the shelf is worthless; what matters is the philosophy lived, the rituals performed, the community actually shown up for.

Religious traditions have an advantage here. They've worked out how to embed meaning in practice. Daily prayers, weekly gatherings, annual festivals, lifecycle rituals: these aren't cultural decoration, they're delivery mechanisms. They take an abstract belief and make it concrete and habitual and embodied.

Secular attempts to replace religion often fail because they focus on beliefs and ignore practices. You can believe in human dignity without any ritual that reinforces it. You can believe in community without gathering. And a belief without a practice withers into an abstraction.

## The stakes

Meaning isn't a luxury. It's a necessity.

Without it, people don't just feel sad. They fall apart. They turn to addiction or fanaticism or despair, and they become easy marks for any demagogue offering purpose. The damage doesn't stay contained to them.

The meaning crisis isn't separate from the mental health crisis or the addiction crisis or the political one. They're symptoms of the same thing: a civilization that dismantled its meaning structures without building replacements.

So the question was never whether you'll have a mythology. You already do. The question is whether you examine it and practice it on purpose, or let it run by default, unexamined, possibly working against you.

Meaning isn't optional. Which one you live by is the part you don't get to skip.
