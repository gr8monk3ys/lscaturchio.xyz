## The illusion of ownership

That music library you've built over a decade? Not yours. The ebooks on your digital shelves are borrowed. The photos you've uploaded, the documents you've stored, the social connections you've spent years on, all of it exists at the pleasure of entities that can revoke access whenever they decide to.

This isn't pessimism. It's contract law.

Read the terms of service for any major platform. Buried in the legalese is a plain fact: you own nothing. You license, you rent, you ask permission to reach what feels like yours but legally isn't. That permission can be withdrawn, sometimes with warning and sometimes without, almost always with no recourse worth the name.

## Peasants on digital land

The medieval peasant understood something modern digital citizens don't. He knew he didn't own the land. He worked the lord's fields and lived in the lord's village and existed at the lord's sufferance. The arrangement was exploitative, but it was at least honest about who held the power.

Platform users don't even get that clarity. The interfaces are built to manufacture a feeling of ownership. "Your" library. "Your" drive. "Your" photos. The possessive is deliberate, and it works: people pour years into building a presence on platforms, never quite registering that they're decorating rented rooms that can be torn down without notice.

And torn down they are, routinely. Accounts get suspended, services shut down, APIs close, whole ecosystems evaporate when a corporate strategy shifts. The graveyard of dead platforms is enormous, and each headstone marks millions of hours of user investment, simply gone.

## The structure of digital property

In physical space, property rights evolved, imperfectly and violently but really, toward some notion of individual ownership. You can own a house, you can own a car. The state might regulate how you use them, but it can't decide you no longer possess them because a quarterly earnings report looked bad.

Digital space grew up differently. The infrastructure is privately owned, the servers and cables and data centers, and through that ownership platforms claim everything that flows across it. Your data lives on their machines, so in a real legal sense it's their data.

This isn't a bug in the system. It's the system. The architecture of consumer technology is built around centralization, not because centralization is technically necessary, since it often isn't, but because it concentrates control. Control over attention, over data, over the terms of engagement. That's where the money is.

## The eviction process

When someone loses access to a platform account, what actually happens?

First, confusion. The interface that was always there simply isn't, and years of accumulated content and connections and context vanish behind a login screen that no longer accepts your credentials.

Then the appeals process, if one exists. Usually it means submitting forms into a void, waiting days or weeks for automated responses that don't address your situation. The asymmetry is total. The platform has infinite patience while you have bills to pay and contacts you can no longer reach.

Sometimes access comes back and sometimes it doesn't, and the reasons are rarely clear. The platform isn't obligated to explain itself. You agreed to terms granting it unilateral authority, so demanding accountability is a little like demanding it from the weather.

The part that unsettles me is that people accept it. They rebuild, they start over, they go back to the same platforms that just demonstrated their powerlessness, because the alternatives feel impossible. Where else would they go? The network effects are real, the lock-in is real, the dependency is real.

## The rent extraction

Feudal lords extracted value from peasants through labor obligations and rents. Platform lords extract it through data harvesting and attention capture. Every interaction logged, every preference modeled, every behavior pattern analyzed and monetized. Users pay for "free" services not with money but with surveillance, their lives converted into targeting data and sold to advertisers.

It runs deeper than data extraction, though. Platforms have found they can shape the information environment itself. They control what you see and decide which posts get amplified and which get buried, optimizing the reality their users inhabit not for truth but for the engagement metrics that feed advertising revenue.

This is a power medieval lords could only dream of. They could compel bodies. Platforms work on minds, or try to, and get a little closer every year as the algorithms improve.

## The pretense of choice

"If you don't like it, don't use it."

This answer to platform criticism misunderstands how infrastructure works. Once something becomes essential infrastructure, the way communication and commerce and professional networking now have, opting out stops being a meaningful choice and becomes self-imposed exile.

Can you function professionally today without email, without access to the platforms where your industry actually operates? In theory, sure. In practice the cost of refusal is enormous, and platforms know it. That's the source of the contempt. Where else are you going to go?

The real choice isn't "use platforms or don't." It's "participate in modern economic and social life or don't." That isn't a choice so much as coercion dressed up as consumer preference.

## Beyond individual solutions

Self-hosting helps. Running your own infrastructure and supporting decentralized alternatives helps. I run some of my own infrastructure, and I'll tell you these are mitigations, not solutions. Most people aren't going to administer their own mail servers, and most can't spare the time it takes to escape platform dependency in the first place.

The problem is structural, so the solution has to be too. Platform feudalism persists because the legal framework lets it: property rights in digital space remain undefined or quietly tilted toward platforms, antitrust enforcement has been toothless for decades, and the political class barely grasps the technology, let alone the power it concentrates.

Changing that means treating platforms as what they are, essential infrastructure that shouldn't sit under unaccountable private control. Public ownership, heavy regulation, mandatory interoperability, something else entirely: the specific instrument matters less than naming the problem correctly.

## The stakes

Digital life isn't separate from life. It's where relationships, work, and politics now happen, and whoever controls that space controls an enormous share of human existence.

Right now that control belongs to a handful of private entities accountable to nobody but their shareholders. They write the rules, enforce them, and rewrite them whenever it suits the quarter, and billions of users adapt to whatever gets imposed.

This is feudalism. Not as metaphor, as description.

Whether the arrangement is sustainable isn't really the question; for the lords, of course it is. The question is whether it's acceptable, whether people will keep surrendering sovereignty over their digital lives to entities that regard them as a resource to be mined rather than citizens to be served. Power doesn't tend to relinquish itself. It gets relinquished when enough people decide the arrangement is intolerable.

The first move is just to see it plainly, to strip away the friendly interface and the familiar possessive pronoun and look at the power structure underneath. You don't own your digital life. Until that changes, you're a peasant on someone else's land, and the lord has read the lease more carefully than you have.
