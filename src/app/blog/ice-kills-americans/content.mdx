## The machine eats its own

There's a comfortable fiction Americans tell themselves about papers. If you're a citizen, ICE can't touch you. The system has safeguards. Due process exists.

That fiction kills people.

US citizens get detained by immigration enforcement. They get deported. Some die in detention. Some get dumped in countries they've never lived in, where they don't speak the language, with nothing but the clothes they're wearing. And the system that does this is working as designed.

Designed to process as many bodies as possible, as quickly as possible, with minimal friction. A citizen is noise in the data.

## How it happens

The mechanics are simple. Immigration enforcement operates on suspicion, not proof. An accent. A skin tone. A name that sounds "foreign," or being in the wrong neighborhood at the wrong time. That's enough to start the machine.

Once you're in custody, the burden flips. Now you have to prove you're a citizen, and proving citizenship from inside a detention center is hard in ways that don't occur to you until you try.

Your birth certificate is at home. So is your passport, so is your Social Security card. The facility doesn't give you access to your belongings, and it doesn't give you time. Phone calls are limited, expensive, and often cut off mid-conversation. Your family might not even know where you are.

The system moves fast. Hearings happen within days, sometimes hours, before families can hire lawyers or locate documents. Immigration courts aren't criminal courts: no public defender, no Miranda rights. The constitutional protections Americans assume cover them don't apply here. That's not a bug.

## The bureaucratic grinder

Immigration enforcement is built for speed and volume. Agents have quotas to meet (officially they don't, but they do). Detention facilities get paid per bed filled, and private contractors profit from every day you're locked up. Everyone in the chain has an incentive to keep the machine running and none to slow down and verify your status.

Your agent probably has 30 cases to process today. Your hearing officer has 50. Nobody has time to check whether you're actually deportable; they need to check boxes and move to the next case. If you can't produce documentation immediately, you're presumed deportable. That's the default, and the default does most of the work.

Even when proof of citizenship exists, it gets ignored. Documents get "lost." Translations get delayed. Hearings happen before evidence arrives. The machine has momentum, and momentum doesn't stop because you found your passport.

Courts have reviewed cases where citizens presented birth certificates, passports, and military service records and still got deported. The paperwork was in the file. The deportation order had already been signed.

## You can't lawyer your way out

The assumption is that a good lawyer fixes this. Get representation, show the papers, walk free. That's not how it works.

Most people in immigration detention can't afford lawyers, and immigration cases aren't criminal proceedings, so there's no right to counsel. You hire someone or you represent yourself, from a detention center, where you can't make phone calls or access the internet or review documents.

Even with a lawyer, the timeline defeats you. By the time the lawyer gets the case, reviews the evidence, and files motions, the client is already on a plane. Once you're deported, fighting the case gets exponentially harder: you're in another country, you can't access US courts easily, and the government has no incentive to bring you back. You can win the legal argument and still be in Honduras.

And the legal standard for detention is low enough to make the rest almost academic. An agent's sworn statement that you admitted to being undocumented is enough. It doesn't matter if you didn't say that, or said the opposite. Their word against yours, and their word wins.

## The statistical reality

How often does this happen? Nobody knows. ICE doesn't track it, courts don't track it, and there's no centralized database of citizens detained or deported by immigration enforcement.

What we know comes from lawsuits, journalistic investigations, and advocacy groups piecing together fragments. The fragments are bad enough. Hundreds of documented cases. Probably thousands of undocumented ones, which is its own dark joke. Some people spend weeks in detention, some spend months, a few spend years.

Some never make it back.

Citizens have died in immigration detention. Medical care in these facilities is notoriously bad, with delayed treatment, ignored symptoms, preventable deaths. When you're processed as a deportable alien, your health is a low priority, because you're going to be someone else's problem soon. Citizens have also been deported and died in countries they had no connection to, dumped without money or contacts or any support system.

The machine doesn't apologize. It rarely acknowledges the mistake at all.

## Why this keeps happening

The system isn't broken. Immigration enforcement is designed to process people, not protect rights, and speed and volume are the features.

Accuracy would slow things down. Verification would require resources. Constitutional protections would create friction. None of that serves the purpose, which is deportation. Everything else is secondary, and a system that treats your citizenship as secondary will deport you eventually.

The opacity is the insidious part, and it's deliberate. Detention centers sit in remote locations. Hearings aren't public. Records aren't easily accessible. Families don't know where their people are, lawyers can't find clients, journalists can't get inside. That's structural, not incompetence. If people could see what happens in there, they'd be horrified, so the system makes sure they can't.

## The expansion problem

Right now this mostly happens to people who "look" undocumented: brown people, people with accents, people in immigrant communities. But the machinery doesn't care about your actual status. It cares about volume.

As enforcement expands and quotas climb and the dragnet widens, more citizens get caught. The pool of "suspicious" people grows. The agent who used to question only Spanish speakers starts questioning anyone without ID. The operation that used to focus on border regions starts working nationwide.

The same indifference that deports citizens now will deport more of them then, because the incentive structure doesn't change and neither does the lack of oversight. What changes is scale.

## There is no safety

The lie is that citizenship protects you. Citizenship is a piece of paper, and papers can be ignored, lost, or deemed insufficient by an agent having a bad day.

The only thing that protects you is staying out of the system. Don't get detained, don't get questioned, don't be in the wrong place at the wrong time, don't have the wrong face or the wrong name or the wrong accent. Which is to say: hope. That's not protection. That's luck wearing protection's clothes.

The machine eats whoever falls into its gears. Citizens are supposed to be exempt, but the exemption requires someone to care enough to check, and nobody in the chain has the time. They have quotas to meet. Your papers won't save you, because the machine doesn't read them closely enough to know the difference.
