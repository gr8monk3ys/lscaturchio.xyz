## Most people would rather electrocute themselves than be alone with their thoughts

In 2014, Timothy Wilson and colleagues at the University of Virginia published a study in *Science* that should have alarmed everyone and instead alarmed almost no one. They sat participants in a bare room for 6 to 15 minutes. No phone, no book, nothing to do but think. The only available stimulus was a button that delivered a mild electric shock — one the participants had already said they would pay money to avoid.

67% of men and 25% of women shocked themselves anyway. One man pressed the button 190 times.

People chose physical pain over sitting quietly with their own minds. Wilson's team called the result "striking." That's one word for it.

## The mind that wanders is working

Your brain doesn't turn off when you stop feeding it input. It turns on differently.

Neuroscientist Marcus Raichle identified the *default mode network* (DMN) in 2001: a set of brain regions that become most active precisely when you're not focused on an external task. For years this was considered noise in the data. Why would the brain spend energy when it had nothing to do?

Because it was doing something. The DMN handles autobiographical memory, future simulation, moral reasoning, social cognition, the construction of narrative identity. In plain language, when you're doing nothing, your brain is busy working out who you are and what comes next.

Jonathan Schooler and Jonathan Smallwood at UC Santa Barbara have spent two decades on mind-wandering, and the finding holds up across studies: wandering during incubation periods (the stretches where you step away from a problem) reliably produces more creative solutions than sustained focused attention. The work happens precisely because you've stopped trying so damn hard.

The catch is that the DMN only engages when you give it room. If every idle moment gets a podcast or a scroll, the system never comes online, and you starve the very process that produces insight and self-understanding. We've been starving it for about fifteen years now.

## Infinite content, zero gaps

Think about the last time you did nothing. Not "nothing" as in scrolling while lying down — actual nothing, no inputs, just you and whatever showed up inside your head. If you can't remember, that itself is the answer.

The architecture of modern life is designed to eliminate dead time. Aza Raskin, who invented infinite scroll while working at Mozilla, has spoken publicly about his regret. Before infinite scroll, you hit the bottom of the page and had to make a conscious decision to continue. After it, there was no bottom. There was always more.

That was 2006. Since then, the entire attention economy has been engineered around the same principle: never let the user reach a point where they might stop consuming. YouTube autoplays the next video. TikTok's algorithm asks nothing of you at all. Spotify has the next song queued before you've wondered what it will be. Every gap is an opportunity for capture, and every company is competing to fill it.

The average American checks their phone 144 times a day, per a 2023 survey by Reviews.org — roughly once every seven waking minutes. At that frequency it has stopped being a habit and become a reflex. Each pickup trades a few seconds of boredom for a few seconds of content, and the trade is so small it never registers as one.

## What gets lost

Boredom does specific things that stimulation can't replace.

The first is tolerance for discomfort. Boredom is mildly unpleasant, and sitting with that unpleasantness (not fixing it, not escaping it, just experiencing it) exercises the same psychological muscle you use to persist through a hard conversation or a craving you know you should resist. Killing boredom with a screen trains the opposite reflex: discomfort is an emergency, relief should be immediate. The downstream effects are ugly. Shortened attention spans, reduced frustration tolerance, difficulty with delayed gratification — training outcomes, however much they get moralized as failings.

Creativity, too. Sandi Mann at the University of Central Lancashire found that participants who performed boring tasks (reading phone numbers from a directory) before a creative challenge outperformed controls who hadn't been bored first. Her explanation: boredom creates a *seeking state*. Deprived of external input, the mind starts generating its own — daydreams, unexpected connections. This is the mechanism behind the shower insight and the walk-around-the-block breakthrough. More input was never the missing ingredient.

And self-knowledge, which is harder to measure and probably matters most. You can't know yourself if you never spend time with yourself, and the phone is the most effective self-avoidance device ever invented. It lets you outsource the contents of your mind at any moment. Feel an emotion? Scroll. Notice a thought you'd rather not examine? Check your email. The result is a population that can tell you what's trending on five platforms but not what it actually wants.

## Stimulation paradox

We are the most stimulated generation in history and among the most bored. That sounds like a contradiction until you look at the mechanism.

Lars Svendsen, in *A Philosophy of Boredom*, distinguishes *situational boredom* (there is nothing to do) from *existential boredom* (nothing is worth doing). We've nearly eliminated the first kind. The second is epidemic.

The mechanism is tolerance. The more you scroll, the more scrolling it takes to feel anything. Social media, short-form video, and push notifications train your reward circuitry to expect constant novelty, and when the novelty stops, even for seconds, you feel a void. Most people call that void boredom. It's closer to withdrawal.

The old kind of boredom, the long Sunday afternoon your grandparents knew, was boring because nothing was happening — and because nothing was happening, something could emerge. A thought, or a restlessness that eventually pointed you toward something real. That boredom was generative. The new kind mostly isn't.

## Choosing nothing

What would it look like to practice boredom?

Leave the phone in another room for an hour and don't replace it with a book or a podcast or anything else. Sit with the nothing. Feel the pull, the phantom vibration, the conviction that you're missing something. You aren't.

Walk without headphones. The mind might circle anxieties for a while; that's fine, let it. Eventually it moves on to something you didn't know was in there. That's the DMN doing its job.

Wait in line without reaching for anything. You'll notice the way people hold their bodies, the quality of the light, the strange familiarity of being a person in a place, doing nothing, aware. That was the baseline human experience for ten thousand years. It became exotic in the last decade.

The discomfort you feel in these moments is real, and it's informative. It measures the gap between where you are and where the species was built to operate: long stretches of nothing, punctuated by bursts of activity, not the inverse. Boredom is a capacity, and like any capacity it rebuilds with use — slowly, and not on the first try.

The man in Wilson's study had a chair, a quiet room, fifteen minutes at most, and his own mind for company. Everything required to do nothing. He pressed the button 190 times.
