## You were sold a defanged religion

Meditation doesn't want you to be more productive. It never did. The contemplative tradition mindfulness was extracted from spans 2,500 years of Buddhist philosophy, and it aimed at something far more radical than your quarterly review: the cessation of *dukkha*, of suffering. Seeing through the illusion of a fixed self. Liberation, in the full religious sense of the word.

None of that sells apps. So we got Headspace.

## The great extraction

In 1979, Jon Kabat-Zinn developed Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction at the University of Massachusetts Medical School. The goal was to bring meditation techniques into clinical settings, stripped of their religious framing, so that secular patients could benefit from the practice. A reasonable idea with an unreasonable consequence.

MBSR worked. It reduced chronic pain, lowered cortisol, helped with anxiety and depression. The clinical evidence was real, and corporate America read it the way corporate America reads everything: stressed workers are expensive, meditation makes workers less stressed, therefore meditation is a productivity input.

Google launched "Search Inside Yourself" in 2007. Aetna reported that after implementing workplace mindfulness programs, employees gained an average of 62 minutes of productivity per week, worth about $3,000 per employee per year. The unit of measurement is worth pausing on. Aetna wasn't tracking peace, or clarity about whether the job deserved the hours. It was tracking output.

So a practice designed to dissolve the ego became a tool for sharpening it.

## McMindfulness

Ronald Purser named this *McMindfulness* in his 2019 book of the same title. His argument is blunt: corporate mindfulness is a privatized spirituality, one that pathologizes the individual rather than questioning the system that made them sick.

You're stressed? Meditate. You're burned out from 60-hour weeks? Here's a breathing exercise and a wellness stipend. The 60-hour weeks themselves stay off the agenda.

Deployed this way, mindfulness functions as a pressure valve. It makes intolerable conditions tolerable. A structural problem (exploitative work, a culture that treats humans as resources) gets reframed as a failure of personal regulation. You're not suffering because the system is broken; you're suffering because you haven't learned to manage your response to it. A tidy arrangement, if you're the system.

## The ethics problem

What got cut when mindfulness went corporate was *sila*, the Buddhist ethical framework.

Traditional practice sits within the Eightfold Path, alongside right speech, right action, right livelihood. You can't separate the meditation from the ethics without changing what the meditation is for. Practicing awareness without ethical commitment is like sharpening a knife without asking what you intend to cut. A sniper has incredible focus. So does a Wall Street trader executing layoffs.

The U.S. military figured this out early. The Army's "Mindfulness-based Mind Fitness Training" program, developed by Amishi Jha at the University of Miami, teaches soldiers to maintain attentional focus under stress, and it works: improved working memory, reduced emotional reactivity. In the military's own language, the soldiers become more resilient — resilient, specifically, to the psychological toll of killing people. The Buddha would have had thoughts about this.

## The attention economy's favorite tool

The same tech companies that engineer addictive products also run mindfulness programs for their employees. Facebook, now Meta, has had one in-house for years, and so has Twitter, along with most platforms whose business model depends on hijacking your attention. They fragment your focus for profit, then offer you meditation to glue it back together.

I build software for a living, and I can tell you that nobody inside these companies experiences this as a contradiction. It's a coherent business model: break the thing, then sell the fix. The tobacco industry funding cancer research had more shame.

## What the practice actually asks

Genuine contemplative practice is uncomfortable. It doesn't make you feel better, at least not at first; it makes you feel *more*. It asks you to sit with pain instead of numbing it, to watch the stories you tell yourself until you can recognize them as stories.

The Zen teacher Charlotte Joko Beck described it as "being the moment with no attempt to escape," which is a harder instruction than it sounds.

It's also a threatening one, in a culture built on escape. We escape into work, into consumption, into our phones, into the future, into the past. A practice that asks you to stop escaping is, in the oldest sense, subversive — which is why the corporate version had to be defanged. Real meditation might lead an employee to realize they hate their job. It might reveal that the stress is the product of a system demanding more than any human should give, and that the honest response is refusal rather than resilience. Can't have that.

## The $2 billion cushion

The meditation app market was valued at roughly $2.2 billion in 2024 and is projected to hit $6.4 billion by 2030. Calm, Headspace, Insight Timer, Ten Percent Happier: subscription pricing on what monks gave away for free. And what the subscription mostly buys is comfort. Sleep stories. Guided relaxations. Anxiety management. The sharp edges of contemplative practice (the existential confrontation, the dissolution of self, the ethical reckoning) filed down to a smooth consumer experience.

You can't disrupt your ego with a subscription model.

## A practice worth reclaiming

None of this means meditation doesn't work. It does. The neuroscience is solid: regular practice changes the brain, with increased gray matter density in the prefrontal cortex and hippocampus, reduced amygdala volume, altered default mode network activity. Sara Lazar's research at Harvard, Richie Davidson's work at the University of Wisconsin — the data is there.

What the data can't tell you is what the practice is for. If you meditate to be more productive, you're using a telescope as a doorstop. If you meditate to stay calm enough to tolerate the intolerable, you've turned a revolutionary practice into an analgesic.

The harder path, the one the tradition actually describes, is meditating without a goal. Sitting and watching your mind without trying to fix it. Letting awareness do what awareness does, which is reveal things you might not want to see. Maybe that your life needs changing rather than optimizing. Maybe that the discomfort you keep trying to breathe through is information.

Meditation was never supposed to make the cage comfortable. It shows you the cage, and then it leaves you sitting there, looking at the bars.
