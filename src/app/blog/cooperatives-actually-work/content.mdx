## The numbers don't lie

Worker cooperatives have a five-year survival rate of around 80%. Traditional businesses come in at about half that. This isn't a fringe number cherry-picked from some advocacy group; it's been documented again and again, across countries and industries and decades.

So why aren't cooperatives everywhere?

The standard answer runs like this: nice in theory, but they can't scale, they're inefficient, they can't raise capital, and anyway workers don't want the responsibility of ownership. Repeat those enough and they start to sound like settled fact.

They aren't.

## The efficiency myth

The belief that hierarchy is simply more efficient than democracy runs deep. Faster decisions, clearer accountability, fewer hours lost to meetings. It sounds right until you look at how traditional corporations actually run.

Most corporate bureaucracies are staggeringly inefficient. Layers of middle management exist less to improve productivity than to watch workers who have no stake in whether the place succeeds. A good chunk of "management science" is, at bottom, the study of how to make people care who have been given no reason to.

Cooperatives need less of that supervision because the members are already supervising themselves. There's no elaborate scheme to stop shirking when the incentive is built in: you're working for yourself, collectively.

And the endless meetings everyone dreads? Large cooperatives sorted that out decades ago. They elect management, delegate the operational decisions, and keep strategic control for the membership. It's representative democracy, which we worked out for political systems a few centuries back. Pointing it at a workplace isn't some impossible leap.

## The capital problem

Cooperatives do face a real obstacle in raising capital. This is the part of the case the boosters tend to skip. But the obstacle is political, not built into the model.

Traditional corporations can issue stock and promise investors a return extracted from worker productivity. Cooperatives can't, or won't. By design they have no outside shareholders to satisfy, which is the whole point and also a genuine handicap when you're competing for startup money in a system built entirely around outside investment.

Banks tend to be cool toward cooperative lending, and not because cooperatives are bad risks. They're better risks. It's that banks don't understand the model, have no standard way to evaluate a cooperative proposal, and were never trained to treat worker ownership as a serious thing.

A state that wanted to fix this could. Cooperative development banks exist elsewhere. Spain's Mondragon network runs its own financial institutions; Germany has a deep cooperative banking sector. The barrier is a set of choices nobody is required to keep making.

## Why the resistance?

If cooperatives win on almost every metric that matters, survival and worker satisfaction and wage equality and the stability of the communities around them, why do they stay marginal?

Follow the incentives.

Venture capitalists don't fund cooperatives because there's no extracted return to take. Business schools don't teach cooperative management because the curriculum is built for corporate recruiters. Politicians don't champion cooperative policy because their donors are conventional owners who read worker ownership as a threat.

The whole apparatus of business development, the incubators and accelerators and angel networks and MBA programs, is tuned for the extraction model. Cooperatives need a parallel set of institutions, and building those takes resources, which takes political will that isn't there.

Underneath all of it sits an assumption: that capital deserves a return simply for existing. Worker ownership pokes at that directly. If workers can run an enterprise without an external owner taking the surplus, what is the owner contributing? Most people in a position to answer would rather the question stay unasked.

## The labor problem that isn't

One objection deserves a real answer: the claim that workers don't actually want ownership. They want a paycheck and some predictability, not the stress of running the place.

There's something to it. Not everyone wants to be an entrepreneur, and plenty of people would happily never sit through a governance meeting. Workers who've been shut out of decisions for an entire career often don't grab at the chance the first time it's offered.

But that proves less than the critics think. People who've never been trusted with responsibility tend not to reach for it, and that's a fact about how they were treated, not a fact about what they can do.

The cooperatives that invest in member education and ease people into ownership don't report the problem. What looks like an aversion to ownership is mostly the residue of an employment system that spent years training people out of expecting any.

## Scaling isn't the problem

Mondragon employs over 80,000 people. It's one of the largest corporations in Spain, working across manufacturing, retail, and finance, and it's been doing it since the 1950s.

The cooperative dairy sector holds real market share in several countries. Credit unions serve over 300 million people worldwide. Housing cooperatives put affordable homes in every major city.

The "cooperatives can't scale" line just ignores the cooperatives that have, mistaking unfamiliarity for impossibility.

What cooperatives don't do is scale the way venture capital wants them to: exponential growth on a timeline built to enrich early investors at exit. They grow steadily instead, reinvesting in workers and the places they operate, refusing the grow-or-die logic that pulls ordinary corporations into the cycle of mergers and layoffs and consolidation. That's read as a weakness. It's the point.

## What would it take?

Expanding cooperative ownership doesn't mean talking workers into risks they can't afford. It means changing the conditions under which businesses get formed and have to compete.

Public banking that understands cooperative finance. Legal reform that makes a cooperative simpler to incorporate. Technical assistance on par with what small business development centers already offer. Procurement preferences that reward worker ownership, and tax treatment that handles cooperative profit-sharing as something other than a dividend. None of it is utopian. All of it is running somewhere right now.

So the question isn't whether the policies work, because they demonstrably do. The question is whether there's the political will to pass them over the objections of the people doing well under the current setup. That's a political question, not an economic one, which is exactly why it rarely comes up in an economics department.

## The actual alternative

Cooperatives won't solve everything. They won't fix the climate on their own, they won't end discrimination, and anyone selling them as a whole political program is overselling. They're a way to organize a firm, nothing larger.

But they settle one thing worth settling: extraction isn't the only way to run a productive enterprise. Workers can manage themselves, democratic governance can coordinate complicated work, and a firm can survive and grow without an outside owner taking the surplus. We have eighty thousand people at Mondragon as the proof.

That this still has to be demonstrated, that the default assumption remains worker incapacity until capital supervises it, tells you how much ideology is loaded into a discourse that calls itself neutral. Cooperatives work, and they have for seventy years. The open question is why we keep pretending they don't.
