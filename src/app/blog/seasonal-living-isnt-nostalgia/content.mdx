## Your body already knows

Every winter you want to sleep more. You crave heavier food, your energy dips, you'd rather curl up somewhere warm than go out.

Every spring something shifts. You want to move, clean, start things. The energy comes back without you asking for it.

This isn't a bug. It's how humans are supposed to work.

Modern life tells you to ignore it. Same wake-up time year-round, same expectations, same schedule whether it's blazing July or dark December. The fluorescent lights don't change with the seasons, so why should you.

## The industrial override

Seasonal variation was a problem for capitalism. Factories can't sit idle for months, supply chains need predictability, and workers who slow down in winter create scheduling headaches.

So we engineered seasonality out. Electric lighting extended the workday past sunset. Climate control made indoor environments identical year-round. The office in January feels exactly like the office in August, on purpose.

And we adapted. Sort of.

The body didn't get the memo about the industrial revolution. Circadian rhythms still track light exposure, melatonin production still shifts with day length, hormone levels still move with the season. The result is a constant low-grade war between what your body wants and what the calendar demands.

## The exhaustion epidemic

Why do people get sick more in winter? The standard answer is viruses spreading in close quarters, which is true enough. But there's something underneath it: chronic circadian disruption, plus the sustained stress of fighting your body's natural downshift, weakens immune function.

Winter is when the body wants to rest and repair. We demand it perform at summer levels instead. We pump ourselves full of caffeine, blast artificial light until midnight, and feel guilty about wanting nine hours of sleep when it's dark at five p.m. Then we're shocked when we crash.

## What seasonal living actually means

Nobody's suggesting we return to subsistence farming and die of smallpox. This isn't about romanticizing pre-modern life or pretending electricity was a mistake. Seasonal living means acknowledging biological reality inside modern constraints, working with your body instead of against it.

In winter, expect less. Sleep more, schedule fewer obligations, lean toward finishing and reflecting rather than starting. Eat warming, substantial food, and use the darkness for rest instead of fighting it. Spring inverts that: ride the rising energy, start the projects, clean out the closet, let the restlessness go somewhere. Summer is the peak, the time to fill the calendar and tackle the demanding work while the daylight is long, with one caveat, which is to notice when you've overextended before the body does it for you. Autumn is the wind-down. Harvest what spring started, finish what's open, and build reserves for the cold.

None of this requires quitting your job or moving to a commune. It's adjustment at the margin. Bed earlier in December. Fewer commitments in January. The ambitious launch scheduled for April instead of February.

## The counter-arguments

"But I live somewhere without real seasons."

Even in temperate climates, day length changes a lot. Your body registers it whether or not you do. And most "seasonless" places still run wet/dry cycles or temperature shifts that the biology picks up on.

"My job doesn't allow seasonal variation."

Fair. Most jobs don't. But you control your non-work hours, your sleep schedule, your weekends, what you say yes to. There's more give there than people assume, even inside rigid employment.

"This sounds like an excuse for laziness."

That's the productivity cult talking, the reflex that treats any accommodation of human limits as excuse-making. Working with your biology rather than against it can raise your total output over a year even if the winter months run slower. The slow months are paying for the fast ones.

"I don't feel seasonal variation personally."

Spend more time outside. Most people who say this live indoors under climate control so completely that they've lost the signal. A few weeks of morning sun and dark evenings usually brings it back.

## The light problem

Artificial light is the biggest disruptor. We flood our evenings with blue-spectrum light from screens and LEDs, the exact wavelengths that suppress melatonin and tell the brain it's midday, and then wonder why sleep won't come.

Winter makes it worse. The body expects darkness to signal rest, but the darkness never arrives, so biological night gets pushed later and later. Morning comes, biologically speaking, while it's still black outside and the alarm is already going off. Chronic circadian misalignment, every winter, for years.

The fix isn't complicated. Cut bright light after sunset, especially blue. Get outside in morning light when you can. Let the evening go dark. Your winter mood and energy improve after that, not perfectly, but enough to feel.

## Against artificial consistency

Industrial society values consistency. Same product every time, same performance every day, same output month after month.

Biological systems don't work that way. They oscillate: active and rest, growth and dormancy. Forcing constant output means supplying constant inputs (caffeine, artificial light, willpower), and that runs down the reserves it draws on.

So replace consistency with rhythm. High-output stretches funded by genuine rest. Sprints, then recovery. Summers of intensity, winters of consolidation. Athletes already build their training in cycles of effort and recovery because that's what produces adaptation; constant maximum effort produces injury. Knowledge work isn't exempt from this, though we keep acting like it is.

## The deeper issue

Seasonal living challenges an assumption that runs deep: that you should be able to override biology with willpower and technology, that the body is a machine to be managed rather than an organism to live in.

You can see the assumption in how we treat sleep, as optional and reducible, and in how we treat food, as fuel to be optimized. Rest becomes unproductive time to be minimized. The body turns into a problem to be solved rather than a home to inhabit.

Aligning with the seasons is one way to push back. It says biological reality matters, that you're an animal, a sophisticated one, but still subject to rhythms you didn't choose and can't fully escape.

## Practical steps

Start by noticing the seasonal patterns in your own energy and mood. Track them for a year if you want the data.

Move your sleep schedule with the season; even thirty minutes registers. Shift the balance of what you take on, weighting projects and social time toward spring and summer, rest and reflection toward fall and winter. Get outside in natural light, mornings especially, since even brief exposure helps calibrate the clock. Cut artificial light at night, most of all in winter when the contrast matters. And when you can choose, put the demanding travel and launches in the higher-energy months.

## Not nostalgia

People dismiss seasonal living as nostalgia because it points back at a pre-industrial pattern. But the argument isn't that the old ways were better. It's that biology changes slower than technology, and fighting biology costs something.

You don't have to romanticize the past to notice that electric light doesn't switch off photosensitive hormones, that climate control doesn't deactivate circadian genes, that expecting the same performance in December and June ignores how the body runs. This isn't going backward. It's taking what chronobiology already established and acting on it, which is a strange thing to have to argue for.

Your ancestors lived seasonally because the environment forced them to. You don't have that excuse, and you don't have their constraint either. The body keeps its own calendar whether or not you consult it.
