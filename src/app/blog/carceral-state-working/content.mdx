## Working as designed

The word "broken" does a lot of work in American political discourse. The immigration system is broken. Healthcare is broken. Ask anyone about criminal justice and you'll hear it a third time.

The word smuggles in a history. It says something once functioned properly and has since deteriorated, and that the right repair (better funding, smarter policy, more training) could restore it. Good intentions gone wrong, fixable by better intentions.

But suppose the criminal justice system isn't broken. Suppose mass incarceration, grotesque racial disparities, sky-high recidivism, and the dehumanization of millions of people are not malfunctions but features — the system doing exactly what it was built to do, just not what it claims to do. That sounds like provocation. The evidence reads more like a job description.

## The exception clause

Start with the text. The Thirteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution, ratified in 1865, abolished slavery and involuntary servitude "except as a punishment for crime whereof the party shall have been duly convicted."

Except as a punishment for crime. The amendment that ended slavery carries a loophole large enough to drive a prison-industrial complex through, and Southern states began driving almost immediately. The Black Codes of the late 1860s criminalized vagrancy, loitering, and breach of labor contracts, offenses written to ensnare formerly enslaved people. Convict leasing, the practice of renting prisoners to private businesses, became the primary mechanism for keeping a coerced Black labor force after emancipation.

The through-line from convict leasing to modern prison labor is direct and documented. Prisoners in the United States today produce goods and provide services for government agencies and private companies, usually for pennies per hour; a 2022 ACLU report puts the average prison wage between $0.14 and $1.41. In several states, incarcerated workers receive no compensation at all.

A system extracting labor from a captive population at below-market rates, continuously, in one form or another, since 1865. Whatever you want to call that, "broken" doesn't describe it.

## The new Jim Crow

Michelle Alexander's *The New Jim Crow*, published in 2010, made an argument that was controversial at the time and is difficult to seriously contest now: mass incarceration functions as a system of racial control that operates through formally race-neutral mechanisms.

The War on Drugs is the clearest example. John Ehrlichman, Nixon's domestic policy advisor, explained the design to journalist Dan Baum, who published the admission in a 2016 *Harper's* article: "We knew we couldn't make it illegal to be either against the war or Black, but by getting the public to associate the hippies with marijuana and Blacks with heroin, and then criminalizing both heavily, we could disrupt those communities."

The sentencing law that followed did the disrupting. Under the 100-to-1 crack-to-powder cocaine ratio in effect from 1986 to 2010, possessing five grams of crack carried the same mandatory minimum sentence as possessing 500 grams of powder cocaine. Pharmacologically, crack and powder cocaine are the same drug. The difference was who used which form, and that difference tracked almost perfectly along racial lines.

The Fair Sentencing Act of 2010 reduced the disparity to 18-to-1, which is the kind of number you arrive at when you've conceded the injustice but not the machinery. It wasn't retroactive. Thousands of people stayed in prison under sentences the government itself had acknowledged were unjust.

## The numbers

The United States incarcerates more people than any other country on Earth, in absolute numbers and per capita. Roughly 1.9 million people are in prison or jail on any given day — about 664 per 100,000 residents. Canada is at 104. Germany, 69. Japan, 38.

Black Americans are incarcerated at nearly five times the rate of white Americans. For drug offenses specifically, despite roughly equal rates of drug use across racial groups, Black people are arrested at 3.73 times the rate of white people, according to ACLU analyses of FBI data.

The recidivism numbers do the most damage to the system's own story. Within five years of release, roughly 66% of formerly incarcerated people are rearrested; within nine years, it's 83%. A system that rehabilitated people would not watch five out of six of them come back.

And if the goal were public safety, you'd expect incarceration rates to track crime rates. They don't. Crime in the U.S. peaked in the early 1990s and has declined significantly since, while the incarceration rate kept climbing until 2009 and has come down only modestly. The Brennan Center for Justice estimates that increased incarceration accounted for roughly zero percent of the crime decline in the 2000s.

So the system doesn't reduce crime, doesn't rehabilitate, costs roughly $81 billion per year in direct expenditures alone, and wrecks families and communities along the way. How does something this expensive and this ineffective at its stated purpose persist?

## The political economy of cages

Ruth Wilson Gilmore's *Golden Gulag* examines California's prison boom from the 1980s through the 2000s and finds the economic logic underneath it. Prisons went up in economically depressed rural areas that had lost agricultural jobs and needed a replacement employer. Construction meant guards, administrators, contractors, suppliers: payrolls in towns with few other options.

That creates a constituency for incarceration. Prison guard unions became major political donors in California, backing tough-on-crime candidates and opposing sentencing reform; the California Correctional Peace Officers Association spent over $100 million on political campaigns between 1998 and 2018. A town that depends on a prison for employment has a direct economic stake in keeping it full.

Then add the private operators. CoreCivic and GEO Group, the two largest private prison companies, generate billions in annual revenue, and their business model depends on a steady supply of incarcerated bodies. They lobby for mandatory minimums, three-strikes laws, expanded immigration detention. Many of their government contracts carry occupancy guarantees, typically 80-90% — if the prison population drops, taxpayers pay for the empty beds.

Step back far enough and the shape is hard to miss: economic interests at every level, arranged so that mass incarceration continuing is the good outcome for someone. The dysfunction is the business model.

## Collateral consequences

Incarceration doesn't end at the prison gate. In most states a felony conviction means losing the right to vote, temporarily or permanently depending on the jurisdiction. An estimated 4.6 million Americans are disenfranchised this way, according to the Sentencing Project, and in several states that population exceeds the margin of victory in recent elections. Removing millions of disproportionately Black and brown citizens from the electorate has effects nobody involved can claim to be surprised by.

Employment discrimination against formerly incarcerated people is legal in most contexts. Housing discrimination is rampant, and access to public benefits is restricted. What remains is a permanent underclass — people who have nominally served their sentence but stay punished for the rest of their lives. And when the conditions of that punishment make reoffending more likely, the system gets to process them again.

## What "reform" misses

Most criminal justice reform assumes the system is broken and needs fixing. More training for police, better prison conditions, sentencing reform at the margins, reentry programs. None of this is worthless; any reduction in suffering matters. But it misdiagnoses the problem. If the system is working as designed (extracting labor, controlling populations, generating revenue, holding political power in place), then reforms that leave the basic structure intact will be absorbed and neutralized. A fresh coat of paint doesn't change what a building was framed to do, and this one has performed its function with remarkable consistency for over 150 years.

Calling the system broken is comfortable. It lets you believe the people running it share your values and just need better tools. Calling it working means admitting that the values embedded in it are not the values we claim to hold, and I'll be honest that I don't know what repair looks like at that scale, or whether "repair" is even the right word for replacing a foundation.

What I keep coming back to is the clause. Fourteen words in the amendment that abolished slavery, ratified in 1865, never repealed, still doing exactly what they were written to allow.
