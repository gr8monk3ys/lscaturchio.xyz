## The past you miss never happened

Nobody is nostalgic for the actual past. What people miss is a feeling, usually some version of stability or belonging, projected backward onto a time they've simplified beyond recognition. This is human and mostly harmless. It is also one of the most reliable tools in the authoritarian playbook.

"Make America Great Again" is not a policy proposal. It's a mood. It works because it doesn't specify which America, or which era, or great for whom. The listener fills in the blank with whatever they've lost: economic security, cultural dominance, the feeling that the world made sense. The slogan doesn't need to be historically coherent, because it was never operating on the level of history.

Nor is this an American specialty. The same move shows up in India, Hungary, Russia — different lost golden ages, same grammar.

## Two kinds of longing

Svetlana Boym, the literary theorist who wrote *The Future of Nostalgia* before her death in 2015, drew the distinction that makes most of this legible. She identified two types of nostalgia: *restorative* and *reflective*.

Reflective nostalgia is bittersweet. It dwells in the longing itself, knows the past is gone, and finds meaning in the distance between then and now. It's the nostalgia of the exile who can't go home but keeps the memory alive anyway, and it tends to produce memoirs and melancholy jokes rather than movements.

Restorative nostalgia wants to rebuild the thing, not mourn it. The lost homeland stops being a memory and becomes a blueprint. And because the past being restored never existed as remembered, the project requires coercion sooner or later: the messy, pluralistic present has to be made to conform to a fantasy of how things used to be.

Every authoritarian movement of the last century has run on the restorative kind.

## The pattern

Reagan's "Morning in America" sold a vision of the 1950s: nuclear families, manufacturing jobs, churches full on Sunday, America as unambiguous global leader. It omitted segregation, McCarthyism, women locked out of professional life, and a Cold War that came close to nuclear annihilation more than once. The actual 1950s were terrifying. The remembered ones are a Norman Rockwell painting.

Modi's Hindu nationalism sells *Bharat*, an ancient, unified Hindu civilization that predates and supersedes the pluralistic, secular republic established in 1947. The *Ram Mandir* in Ayodhya turns that history into architecture. Sustaining the picture means erasing centuries of syncretic culture, ignoring how internally diverse Hinduism is (to the point of incoherence as a single tradition), and recasting the Mughal period as pure foreign oppression rather than the complicated, culturally productive entanglement it actually was.

Orban's Hungary runs on the Treaty of Trianon, the 1920 settlement that stripped Hungary of two-thirds of its territory after World War I. A century later, Orban's government still puts pre-Trianon maps on official materials. The message: Hungary was great, foreigners diminished it, and only Fidesz can restore what was taken. That the pre-Trianon Kingdom of Hungary was a multiethnic empire in which Magyars were barely a majority goes unmentioned.

Putin's Russia sells the Soviet Union minus the communism — the superpower status, the global influence, the empire from the Baltic to the Pacific. Also minus the gulags, the famines, the repression, and the basic economic dysfunction that collapsed the whole thing.

The recipe barely varies. Take a real loss and attach it to a mythologized past. Offer yourself as the vehicle of restoration. Then name the enemies, usually minorities or foreigners or cosmopolitan elites, who supposedly ended the golden age.

## Why it works

Nostalgia is, among other things, a threat response. Research by Constantine Sedikides and Tim Wildschut at the University of Southampton has shown that nostalgia increases when people feel uncertain, lonely, or existentially threatened. It works as a coping mechanism: when the present feels fragmented, the remembered past supplies continuity and meaning. In experimental settings, inducing nostalgia makes people feel warmer (literally: they estimate the room temperature as higher), more socially connected, and more certain of life's meaning.

This is why economic dislocation is such fertile ground for nostalgic politics. When your factory closes, your town hollows out, and your kids can't afford to live where they grew up, the loss is real, and the longing for a time when things worked, or seemed to, is completely rational.

The manipulation comes in the diagnosis. Nostalgic politicians don't say "global capital restructured the economy in ways that benefited shareholders at the expense of workers." They say foreigners took your jobs, or the elites sold you out, or we lost our way when we stopped being who we were. And the cure on offer is a direction rather than a mechanism: go back, far enough that the problem hasn't happened yet.

Except there is no back. The conditions that created the remembered stability (postwar economic expansion, Cold War military spending, cheap energy, racial and gender hierarchies that suppressed competition) are gone. The factory jobs aren't coming back because the factories aren't coming back, because the economic logic that sustained them no longer exists. Telling people you'll restore the 1950s economy without restoring the 1950s conditions is a lie. But it's a lie that *feels* true, and feeling is what nostalgia runs on.

## The things that actually changed

The weapon gets its grip from a true premise: some things genuinely were better in the past, for certain populations.

Real wages for American workers without college degrees peaked in the early 1970s. Union membership, and the bargaining power it conferred, has been declining for fifty years. Housing costs relative to income have skyrocketed. Healthcare, education, and childcare all consume a larger share of household budgets than they did a generation ago. Social mobility has stalled.

These are legitimate grievances. People who feel economically squeezed aren't imagining it. But the nostalgic framing attributes the losses to cultural change (immigration, secularism, feminism, the dissolution of traditional hierarchies) rather than to the policy choices that actually caused them: deregulation, tax restructuring, the long dismantling of organized labor, the financialization of the economy.

Nostalgic politics takes real economic pain and redirects the anger toward scapegoats. The bait-and-switch is effective enough that people vote, enthusiastically, for tax cuts for the wealthy, deregulation of industry, and weaker labor protections — the very policies that built the conditions they want to escape.

## Nostalgia vs. memory

None of this means the past is useless. Some things are worth remembering, even worth recovering. The commons, mutual aid, the expectation that public institutions should serve the public — these have actual histories, and the histories can inform actual politics.

The difference is between *reflective* engagement with the past and *restorative* mythologizing. Reflective engagement says: here's something that worked, here's why it stopped working, here's how we might adapt it. Restorative mythologizing says: everything was perfect, enemies destroyed it, give me power and I'll bring it back. One is historical thinking. The other is a con.

You can ask the standard questions when a politician tells you things used to be better. Better for whom, and when, exactly? What are you actually proposing to do about it? They're worth asking. Just don't expect them to break the spell, because the spell was never factual to begin with. You can't fact-check homesickness, least of all homesickness for a home that never existed, and the loss underneath it stays real whether or not anyone names it honestly. Which may be why, a hundred years on, the old maps keep appearing on Hungarian government materials: the Kingdom intact, two-thirds larger than the country printing it, long gone and still on the letterhead.
