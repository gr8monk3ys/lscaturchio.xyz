## Your body has a score now

You slept 7 hours and 23 minutes last night. Your sleep score was 74. Your REM was below average. Your resting heart rate was 58, which is good, but your heart rate variability was 34, which is below your baseline. You took 8,247 steps yesterday, falling short of your 10,000-step goal. Your screen time was 4 hours and 12 minutes, up 17% from last week. Your readiness score is 68. Your body battery is at 43%.

How do you feel?

That's a trick question. It doesn't matter how you feel. The numbers have already told you how you're doing.

## The measurement changes the thing

In 2016, Jordan Etkin, a researcher at Duke University, published a study that should have made every fitness tracker manufacturer nervous. She found that measuring an activity reduced the enjoyment people derived from it. Participants who tracked their walking reported less pleasure than those who didn't, even when they walked the same amount. Measurement turned walking from something you do into something you perform. The number on the screen became the point, and the walk became a means to it.

This is the Hawthorne effect turned inward. The original Hawthorne studies, run at a Western Electric factory in the 1920s, found that workers' productivity changed simply because they knew they were being watched. The observation altered the behavior. What Etkin showed is that self-observation does the same. Measure your own experience and you're no longer a person going for a walk. You're a person generating step data.

That shift, from experiencing to measuring, reorders how you relate to your own body.

## Orthosomnia: losing sleep over sleep scores

In 2017, researchers at Rush University Medical Center and Northwestern University coined the term *orthosomnia* to describe a new clinical phenomenon: patients who were developing sleep disturbances as a direct result of obsessing over their sleep tracker data. People were lying awake worrying about whether they were getting enough deep sleep. They'd check their scores in the morning and, if the numbers were bad, feel tired regardless of how they actually felt before looking at the screen.

The researchers, led by Kelly Glazer Baron, documented cases where patients' self-reported sleep quality worsened after they started using trackers, even as their objective sleep (measured by polysomnography) stayed the same or improved. The tracker wasn't revealing a problem. It was creating one.

This gets at something the quantified self movement would rather not confront. These devices aren't neutral instruments. They're interpretation machines. They take continuous biological signals, run them through proprietary algorithms, and hand back a number. That number feels authoritative, it's data after all, but it's an abstraction sitting on top of a much messier reality. Your body doesn't have a sleep score. Whoop gave you one.

And once you have the number you can't unknow it. You can't simply feel rested anymore. You have to *be* rested, as defined by an algorithm you're not allowed to inspect.

## The dashboard self

Deborah Lupton, a sociologist at the University of New South Wales, has written extensively about the *quantified self* as a cultural phenomenon. In *The Quantified Self*, she argues that self-tracking technologies don't simply record our lives; they produce a specific kind of self, one that is knowable through data and whose value is legible in metrics.

This is a real shift in what it means to be a person. For most of human history self-knowledge was qualitative. You knew yourself through reflection and relationships and the accumulated evidence of how things felt. The quantified self swaps that out for a regime of numbers. You don't know how you feel. You know what your HRV is.

Gina Neff and Dawn Nafus, in their book *Self-Tracking*, add a related point. Self-tracking devices don't just measure; they *normalize*. They establish baselines, set acceptable ranges, flag the deviations, and the standard they hold you to comes from population-level data that may have nothing to do with your particular body.

When your tracker says your VO2 max is "below average," it's comparing you to everyone else in its dataset. It knows nothing about your asthma, your age, the altitude you live at. The number still feels like a verdict, and you internalize it.

## Surveillance marketed as self-knowledge

A device that monitors your heart rate, sleep, location, activity, and stress around the clock is a surveillance device. That you bought it yourself and it reports to your phone rather than a government database doesn't change the category.

Apple, Google, Fitbit, Oura, Whoop, all of them sit on your biometric data behind privacy policies that are long and lawyered. The data is "anonymized," which means roughly what it sounds like: roughly. Researchers have shown repeatedly that anonymized health data can be re-identified once you combine it with other sources.

The privacy problem is real, but it's almost secondary to the psychological one. The more insidious surveillance is the kind you run on yourself. You've installed a panopticon on your wrist and taken both jobs, watcher and watched, and the watching never stops.

Michel Foucault would have had a field day. The disciplinary power he described operates not through force but through constant visibility, through the way you eventually carry the observer's gaze around inside you, and that's precisely what these devices enable. The difference is the source. No prison guard, no supervisor. Just a widget on your home screen.

## The tyranny of the streak

Streaks are the gamification layer bolted on top of the surveillance layer. Close your rings. Hit your goal. Don't break the chain.

They exploit a well-documented vulnerability: loss aversion. We hate losing progress more than we enjoy making it. A 30-day meditation streak doesn't make you want to meditate on day 31. It makes you *terrified* of not meditating on day 31. The motivation isn't positive, it's anxious.

People exercise while sick rather than break a streak. People stress about their stress scores, which should be disqualifying for the whole industry on its own, and check their sleep data in the morning before they've registered how they actually feel, letting the number speak first.

At what point does self-improvement become self-harassment?

## What gets lost

When every dimension of your life has a metric, the ones that don't start to feel optional. How do you quantify a good conversation? What's the score for feeling at peace, or for whether you were actually present with your kids at dinner versus parked in the chair reviewing your activity stats?

The quantified self doesn't only measure what it measures. It quietly insists that what it measures is what counts. Steps count, calories count, sleep stages count, and the unmeasurable, contentment and meaning and depth of experience, drifts to the edge of the frame. Not because you decided it was less important, but because the dashboard has no field for it.

This is the logic the philosopher Ian Hacking calls *making up people*. The categories we invent don't just describe the world; they reshape it. Define a person by their metrics and you produce a person who sees themselves through metrics. The tool shapes the user.

## The alternative is harder

I'm not arguing for ignorance. If you have a heart condition, monitor your heart rate; if you're training for a marathon, track your mileage. Measurement has its place when it's specific, bounded, and in service of a defined goal.

That's not what the movement sells, though. It sells total legibility, the promise that if you collect enough data about yourself you'll finally understand yourself, that self-knowledge is an engineering problem waiting on better sensors.

It isn't. Self-knowledge is a human problem. It means sitting with uncertainty and sometimes accepting that you don't know why you feel the way you feel, and that being okay.

You're not a dashboard or a score or a streak.

Take the watch off once in a while and go for a walk. Don't count the steps.
